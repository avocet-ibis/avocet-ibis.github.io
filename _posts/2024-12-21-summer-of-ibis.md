---
title: Summer of the Ibis
---

The White-faced Ibis (*Plegadis chihi*) is a familiar friend to birders throughout Southern Wyoming. During summer, hundreds can be seen nesting in the bulrush and foraging along the shallow alkaline waters of Hutton Lake National Wildlife Refuge, located 10 miles southwest of Laramie, WY. These waders stand tall on a pair of red legs and carry a long, decurved bill to probe mud for food. They appear black from a distance, but when close up, their plumage transforms into a deep glossy red, and their iridescent wings shimmer metallic green and bronze. During the breeding season, adults sport bright pink patches of bare skin in front of their red eyes, bordered by a narrow line of white feathers (hence the name).

![White-faced Ibis]({{ site.baseurl }}/images/2024-12-21/white-faced.jpg "White-faced Ibis")

This gregarious species migrates in large numbers and will form nesting colonies as they settle for the breeding season. At Hutton Lake, they mostly feed in open water but nest westward in a large, heavily vegetated floodplain that recently became part of the Wyoming Toad Conservation Area. They are often seen flying between the two areas, with rapid wingbeats and necks and legs outstretched. While overhead, they frequently vocalize with low croaking and grunting calls, generally the only sounds you can expect to hear from this quiet species.

There are two other Ibis common to the United States: Glossy Ibis (*Plegadis falcinellus*) and White Ibis (*Eudocimus albus*). Both have a restricted range compared to the White-faced Ibis, which can be found throughout the western interor and along the Pacific and Gulf Coasts. In the States, the Glossy Ibis tends to the Eastern Gulf Coast, Florida, and the Atlantic Coast; the White Ibis has a similar range but can't be found as far north and more often appears inland throughout the south. The Cornell Lab of Ornithology provides excellent range maps for [White-faced Ibis](https://www.allaboutbirds.org/guide/White-faced_Ibis/maps-range), [Glossy Ibis](https://www.allaboutbirds.org/guide/Glossy_Ibis/maps-range), and [White Ibis](https://www.allaboutbirds.org/guide/White_Ibis/maps-range).
